\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought supervision is usually written in natural language, even when
the latent reasoning problem is symbolic. We ask whether the same models learn
more correct reasoning when the chain of thought is written in a compact formal
logic instead. We introduce two controlled synthetic reasoning suites,
\branchproof{} and \attrcon{}, where each example has paired formal-logic and
natural-language derivations generated from the same latent proof. This lets us
change the reasoning substrate while holding the underlying problem fixed.
Across depth extrapolation, shortcut-rich training, syntax controls, hybrid
formal--natural traces, and architecture ablations, compact formal traces
consistently improve answer correctness. On \branchproof{} with OLMo-7B,
logic-trained models reach 92.1\% held-out correctness and 83.3\% depth-50
correctness, compared with 79.4\% and 51.0\% for natural-language traces. On
\attrcon{}, the gap is largest at depth 50: 45.8\% versus 10.4\%. The effect is
not explained by simply making traces longer, more explicit, or more
``code-like'': wordified logic, symbol-padded logic, pseudocode, rule-annotated
natural language, and dual-modality training all underperform compact logic. At
the same time, validity controls show that answer correctness and proof validity
are distinct: invalid formal-looking traces can preserve high correctness while
collapsing mechanical proof validity. Our evidence supports a narrower and more
actionable claim: for small supervised models on controlled compositional tasks,
the syntax and locality of compact formal reasoning can make the correct
computation easier to learn than natural-language chain of thought.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Language models can solve harder problems when they externalize intermediate
reasoning steps~\citep{wei2022chain,kojima2022large,zelikman2022star,zhou2023least}.
Most work treats these steps as natural-language explanations. That choice is
convenient for humans, but it is not obviously optimal for the model: natural
language is verbose, ambiguous, and entangles semantic content with discourse
style. Formal logic has the opposite bias. It is compact, typed by construction,
and makes rule application explicit, but it is less like the text distribution
seen during pretraining.

This paper studies the substrate question directly:
\emph{holding the latent proof fixed, should a small language model be trained
to reason in natural language or in formal logic?} We focus on supervised
fine-tuning rather than inference-time prompting so that the answer reflects
what the model can learn from a substrate, not what a frontier model already
knows how to imitate. The main comparison is therefore not ``symbolic solvers
versus language models'', but \emph{two target languages for the same generated
reasoning process}.

We build paired synthetic datasets where every instance is generated from a
deterministic latent proof and rendered in two ways: a compact formal derivation
and a matched natural-language derivation. The primary suite,
\branchproof{}, contains branching Horn-style proof chains with controlled
depth and optional shortcut distractors. The secondary suite, \attrcon{},
contains attribute-constraint derivations over object slots and values. These
tasks are intentionally narrow: they isolate compositional rule following,
state tracking, distractor rejection, length extrapolation, and final-answer
selection while giving us exact labels and mechanical proof diagnostics.

Our results support four claims.
First, compact formal logic improves answer correctness under depth
generalization. On the main \branchproof{} OLMo-7B sweep, logic is especially
strong when evaluation depth exceeds training depth, reaching 83.3\% depth-50
correctness after training up to depth 25 versus 51.0\% for natural-language
traces.
Second, the effect is not just ``more structure is better''. Pseudocode,
rule-annotated natural language, longer token-matched logic, wordified logic,
and symbol-padded logic do not recover compact logic performance.
Third, combining formal and natural-language reasoning in the same training
target or in separate mode-conditioned examples does not beat compact formal
reasoning alone. Formal-first hybrids are better than natural-language-first
hybrids, but still trail the single-substrate logic baseline.
Fourth, validity remains a separate axis. Invalid or shuffled formal traces
can retain nontrivial answer accuracy, so we do not claim that the model has
learned a fully faithful theorem prover. The robust conclusion is about
\emph{correctness}: compact formal traces make the supervised computation
easier for these models.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Natural-language chain of thought.}
Chain-of-thought prompting shows that writing intermediate rationales can
substantially improve large-model reasoning~\citep{wei2022chain}. Follow-up
work studies zero-shot chain of thought~\citep{kojima2022large}, rationale
bootstrapping~\citep{zelikman2022star}, decomposition into easier
subproblems~\citep{zhou2023least}, and search over alternative reasoning
states~\citep{yao2023tree}. These methods mostly preserve natural language as
the reasoning medium. We instead ask whether a model trained on the same
underlying problem learns more reliably when the intermediate reasoning is
rendered in a formal symbolic language.

\paragraph{Programs and executable substrates.}
Program-aided and program-of-thought methods use code as an external reasoning
substrate, often delegating arithmetic or symbolic manipulation to an
interpreter~\citep{gao2023pal,chen2023program}. This line demonstrates that
alternative substrates can improve reasoning, but usually changes both the
trace language and the execution mechanism. Our experiments keep inference as
plain next-token generation and vary only the supervised trace surface, so the
comparison isolates what the model learns to emit.

\paragraph{Logic translation and symbolic solvers.}
Several systems translate language into a formal representation and call an
external solver, verifier, or theorem prover~\citep{pan2023logiclm,ling2023deductive}.
This can improve faithfulness and correctness, but the solver performs part of
the reasoning. Our setting is complementary: the model must generate both the
intermediate derivation and the final answer, while validators are used only
for evaluation.

\paragraph{Formal mathematics and theorem proving.}
Lean- and Isabelle-based LLM systems train or guide models to produce formal
proofs, often for mathematical theorem proving~\citep{polu2020generative,
jiang2022draft,leandojo2023,leanCopilot2024}. These works show the value of
machine-checkable reasoning languages, but the task domain is specialized and
the goal is often proof search. We use much smaller synthetic tasks to ask a
more basic representational question: when the same proof can be written as
logic or natural language, which substrate better supports learned correctness?

\paragraph{Shortcut learning and controlled generalization.}
Shortcut learning describes the tendency of models to exploit superficial
regularities that work in training but fail under distribution
shift~\citep{geirhos2020shortcut}. Synthetic generators let us control those
regularities exactly. Our shortcut ablations train on shortcut-rich data but
evaluate on shortcut-neutral data, testing whether a reasoning substrate makes
the intended rule-following computation easier to recover.

\section{Controlled Reasoning Suites}
\label{sec:datasets}

Each dataset instance has a latent derivation graph, a prompt, a target trace,
and a final answer. The formal and natural-language traces are paired:
they refer to the same facts, rules, intermediate states, and answer. This is
crucial because the experiment is about the substrate, not about different
examples or different reasoning depths.

\paragraph{\branchproof{}: branching Horn proofs.}
\branchproof{} generates a small symbolic world with unary predicates over
entities, Horn-style rules, and a target state that is reachable through a
multi-step branch. Distractor branches are locally coherent but do not support
the target conclusion. The generator controls the maximum proof depth during
training and evaluates on held-out depths, including a depth-50 split far beyond
the training range. The compact logic target is a sequence of cited derivation
lines such as applying a rule to a previous fact to derive a new atom. The
natural-language target states the same derivation in short sentences and ends
with the same answer tag.

\paragraph{\attrcon{}: attribute constraints.}
\attrcon{} uses object attributes rather than abstract predicates. Rules link
named objects, slots, and values; the answer depends on propagating constraints
through a chain of attribute updates. This suite checks whether the logic
advantage survives a more semantic surface where symbols are closer to words
and the model must preserve slot/value bindings. We use \attrcon{} as a
secondary confirmation dataset: it shares the paired rendering principle but
differs in ontology and rule vocabulary.

\paragraph{Why these tasks.}
The suites are not intended to be broad benchmarks. They cover the reasoning
capabilities needed for the paper's causal comparison: compositional rule
application, maintaining intermediate state, rejecting coherent distractors,
extrapolating beyond training depth, and producing a final answer from a trace.
Because the generator is deterministic and paired, we can run fine-grained
ablations that would be ambiguous on natural datasets.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We report pass@16 answer correctness unless otherwise noted. For formal traces,
we also compute mechanical validity and joint correctness-plus-validity. For
natural-language traces, translated validity is available only where the
family-specific translator supports the surface; the main scientific claim is
therefore answer correctness, with validity used as a diagnostic rather than as
a universal metric.

\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Setup.}
The main experiments fine-tune OLMo-7B models on paired \branchproof{} data at
maximum training depths 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25 over three seeds. Evaluation uses
held-out examples from the same family and a sparse depth-50 extrapolation
split. We repeat the main comparison on \attrcon{}, run shortcut-rate controls,
syntax-level ablations, same-datapoint hybrids, mode-conditioned dual training,
and architecture checks on Qwen and Gemma models. All plotted uncertainty bands
or error bars are standard deviations over seeds.

\subsection{Compact Logic Improves Correctness}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/main_correctness.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Formal logic improves correctness on \branchproof{}.}
  OLMo-7B models are trained up to the depth on the x-axis and evaluated on
  held-out depths or depth 50. Lines show three-seed means; bands show standard
  deviation.}
  \label{fig:main-correctness}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:main-correctness} shows the central result. Natural-language
traces eventually reach strong held-out performance at train depth 25, but
logic is consistently better at the harder extrapolation point. At train depth
25, the logic model reaches 92.1\% held-out correctness and 83.3\% depth-50
correctness. The natural-language model reaches 79.4\% and 51.0\%. The effect
is not monotonic in training depth, especially at low depths, but the best
logic-trained models are both more accurate and more depth-robust.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/attribute_correctness.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{The correctness advantage transfers to \attrcon{}.}
  \attrcon{} changes the ontology from abstract proof atoms to object
  attributes and slot/value constraints. Logic remains stronger at the hardest
  depth-50 split.}
  \label{fig:attribute-correctness}
\end{figure}

\attrcon{} gives a second, semantically different check
(Figure~\ref{fig:attribute-correctness}). At train depth 25, logic reaches
89.6\% held-out correctness and 45.8\% depth-50 correctness, while natural
language reaches 81.7\% and 10.4\%. The depth-50 validity metric for \attrcon{}
logic remains harsh, so we treat this as correctness evidence, not as proof
that long formal derivations are fully valid.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.58\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/shortcut_robustness.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Logic is more robust to shortcut-rich training.}
  Training examples contain shortcut markers at the shown rate, but evaluation
  is shortcut-neutral. Depth-50 correctness remains high for formal logic while
  natural-language traces degrade as shortcut rate increases.}
  \label{fig:shortcut}
\end{figure}

The shortcut-rate ablation trains on data where a superficial marker is often
predictive of the correct branch, then evaluates after removing that shortcut.
Figure~\ref{fig:shortcut} shows that formal logic is much less sensitive to
this distributional trap. Across shortcut rates 0.3, 0.5, and 0.8, logic
depth-50 correctness stays near 82--84\%, while natural language falls from
45.8\% to 28.1\%. This supports the interpretation that the compact formal
surface makes the rule-following path easier to learn than the marker heuristic.

\subsection{What Part of Logic Matters?}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.86\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/syntax_controls.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{The gain is not recovered by generic verbosity or structure.}
  All controls are train-depth-25 \branchproof{} runs. Wordifying predicates,
  padding symbols, adding rule annotations to NL, using pseudocode, or using the
  current terse-NL control all underperform compact logic on held-out
  correctness. Error bars show seed standard deviation.}
  \label{fig:syntax}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:syntax} tests whether the logic advantage is merely a proxy for
trace length, explicit rule labels, or code-like formatting. It is not.
Wordified logic replaces compact predicate symbols with natural attribute-like
names while keeping formal proof rules; held-out correctness drops to 50.8\%.
Symbol-padded logic lengthens atoms mechanically and reaches 67.5\%.
Pseudocode and rule-annotated natural language remain below the compact logic
baseline. The current terse-NL control is a weak negative control because the
default natural-language traces are already short; it should not be read as a
clean shorter-NL result. The broad pattern is nevertheless clear: compact
formal syntax is doing more than adding tokens or visible rule names.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.62\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/trace_integrity.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Correctness and formal validity separate.}
  Invalid and shuffled logic traces can preserve answer correctness while
  mechanical joint correctness-plus-validity collapses. The paper therefore
  claims improved correctness from formal traces, not guaranteed faithful proof
  generation.}
  \label{fig:integrity}
\end{figure}

Trace-integrity controls sharpen the claim
(Figure~\ref{fig:integrity}). Invalid logic retains high held-out correctness
but has zero formal joint validity. Shuffled logic also keeps nontrivial
correctness while destroying validity. This is not a failure of the central
correctness result; it is a boundary on its interpretation. Compact formal
traces can make the answer computation easier without forcing the model to emit
a fully checkable proof at every depth.

\subsection{Do Hybrids Help?}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/hybrid_order.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Same-datapoint hybrids do not beat compact logic.}
  Dashed lines are single-substrate baselines; solid square-marker lines train
  on both substrates in the same target. Formal-first hybrids are much better
  than natural-language-first hybrids, but compact logic alone remains the best
  depth-generalizing format.}
  \label{fig:hybrid}
\end{figure}

Hybrid traces contain both a formal block and a natural-language block before
the answer. The order matters. Formal-first hybrids are much stronger than
natural-language-first hybrids, reaching 87.9\% held-out correctness and
64.6\% depth-50 correctness at train depth 25. But this still trails compact
logic alone, which reaches 92.1\% and 83.3\%. Qualitative inspection shows a
plausible failure mode: the model can learn the intended surface, but the
longer target spends context and probability mass on translating or repeating
the same derivation, making long-depth completion more fragile.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.68\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/conditioned_dual.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Mode-conditioned dual training does not recover both
  single-substrate models.} A dual model sees separate formal and
  natural-language examples with a mode token. At train depth 25 it remains
  below the corresponding single-substrate baselines, especially for logic.}
  \label{fig:conditioned}
\end{figure}

Conditioned dual training removes the same-datapoint length issue: the model
sees separate examples with a mode token requesting formal logic or natural
language. It still does not catch up
(Figure~\ref{fig:conditioned}). At train depth 25, conditioned logic reaches
83.3\% held-out and 67.7\% depth-50 correctness, below the single logic
baseline. Conditioned natural language reaches 67.5\% and 53.1\%, below the
single natural-language held-out baseline and only slightly above its depth-50
result. This suggests interference and reduced per-substrate exposure, rather
than a simple train-test mismatch.

\subsection{Architecture Check}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{official_preprint/figures/architecture_depth50.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{The depth-50 correctness gap is not OLMo-specific.}
  At train depth 25, formal logic is stronger than natural language across the
  completed OLMo, Qwen, and Gemma architecture checks.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The architecture ablation is not as exhaustive as the OLMo sweep, but it is an
important robustness check. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} compares depth-50
correctness at train depth 25 across completed model families. Logic is above
natural language for OLMo-7B, Qwen-2.5-1.5B, Qwen-2.5-7B, and Gemma-3-4B,
suggesting that the effect is not tied to one tokenizer or one base model.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{A coherent interpretation.}
The strongest reading of these experiments is not that formal logic is
universally superior to natural language. It is that, when a task's latent
structure is symbolic and compositional, compact formal traces can align better
with the computation a small model must learn. They shorten local dependencies,
reduce discourse variation, and make rule application syntactically regular.
This helps answer correctness, especially when evaluation depth exceeds the
training range or when training contains shortcut distractors.

\paragraph{What the ablations rule out.}
The syntax controls argue against several easy explanations. Logic does not win
because it is longer: token-budget matched and padded variants are worse. It
does not win simply because it has explicit rules: rule-annotated natural
language and pseudocode do not catch up. It does not win because any mixture of
formal and natural language is better: both hybrid orders and conditioned dual
training trail compact logic. The most consistent hypothesis is that the
compact symbolic alphabet and citation-like local proof structure jointly make
the supervised mapping easier.

\paragraph{What remains unresolved.}
The validity controls are the main caveat. A model can exploit formal-looking
regularities to get the answer right while emitting an invalid proof. Therefore
formal traces are not automatically faithful explanations. Future work should
separate three objectives: answer correctness, trace validity, and causal
faithfulness of the trace to the answer. Our experiments establish the first
objective most strongly.

\section{Limitations}

The tasks are synthetic and intentionally narrow. They isolate the substrate
comparison but do not prove transfer to open-domain reasoning. The models are
small-to-medium supervised models, not frontier prompting systems. Some
validity metrics are family-specific: natural-language translated validity is
only meaningful where the translator supports the dataset, and \attrcon{}
depth-50 formal validity remains much harsher than correctness. Finally, the
current terse-NL ablation is not a clean independent compression control
because the default natural-language trace is already terse.

\section{Conclusion}

Formal logic can be a better chain-of-thought substrate than natural language
for small supervised models on controlled symbolic reasoning tasks. The key
evidence is correctness: compact logic improves held-out and depth-extrapolated
answers, transfers to an attribute-constraint suite, resists shortcut-rich
training, and remains stronger across architecture checks. The ablations refine
the story: the benefit is not generic verbosity, code-likeness, or hybridization
with natural language. It appears to come from the compact, regular local
structure of the formal trace. That makes formal logic a promising substrate for
training reasoning behavior, while leaving proof validity and faithfulness as
separate targets for future work.
